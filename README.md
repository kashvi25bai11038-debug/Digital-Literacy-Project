#### **Project Overview**




This report details a five-task digital literacy portfolio. Key tasks included designing a Canva infographic , setting up LinkedIn, GitHub, and Kaggle profiles , and passing a HackerRank challenge. It also covers cybercrime awareness regarding UPI fraud and professional email etiquette.





#### **Module Summaries**



Task 1 – Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic (Module 1)

Designed a one-page infographic using Canva covering three key topics:

What Digital Literacy is
Useful internet tools for students
Professional online presence



Task 2 – Student Digital Portfolio (Module 2)

For this task I created and set up accounts on three professional platforms — GitHub, LinkedIn, and Kaggle. On GitHub, I created a public repository named digital-literacy-project and set up a profile README with my name, branch, year, and a learning goal statement. On LinkedIn, I filled in the Education section with my current degree, institution, and expected graduation year, keeping the profile professional and minimal. On Kaggle, I set up a basic profile to begin building familiarity with the data science community, which will become increasingly relevant as my degree progresses. Each platform serves a distinct purpose: GitHub as a technical portfolio, LinkedIn as a professional identity, and Kaggle as a space to practise and showcase data skills over the coming years.



Task 3 – Coding \& Collaboration Platforms (Module 3)

For Part A, I created an account on HackerRank and completed the Solve Me First beginner challenge, which involved writing a function to return the sum of two integers. The solution was accepted and a badge was awarded upon completion. For Part B, I built a five-question Digital Literacy Awareness Quiz using Google Forms, covering topics like cloud tools, coding platforms, and online account safety. The form includes both multiple choice and short answer questions, is linked to a Google Sheet for response tracking, and the link has been added to this README below.



Task 4 – Professional Email \& Etiquette Guide (Module 4)

For this task I drafted two professional emails — one from a student to a professor requesting an assignment deadline extension, and one expressing interest in a summer internship opportunity. Both follow correct email structure including a clear subject line, professional greeting, well-organised body, and a proper sign-off. I also created a Social Media Do's and Don'ts checklist covering responsible online behaviour for college students. As part of the report write-up, I reflected on a real-world case where a single carelessly written social media post caused severe professional consequences, and what the person could have done differently before hitting post.



Task 5 – Cybercrime Awareness Case Study (Module 5)

For this task I researched UPI and Online Payment Fraud — one of the most common cybercrimes targeting college students in India. The case study covers how the fraud typically happens, who is targeted, and what the consequences are. The most surprising finding was how polished and legitimate these scams look, often perfectly mimicking official bank and NPCI alerts. I also created a Stay Safe Online prevention checklist with eight actionable tips, including two specific to UPI and financial safety. The key habit I will personally change is never entering my UPI PIN in response to an incoming payment request, no matter how convincing the message looks. Incidents can be reported at the National Cyber Crime Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) or by calling Helpline 1930.

#### **Conclusion**



Working through all five tasks of this project gave me a much clearer picture of what it actually means to be digitally literate — not just knowing how to use a phone or laptop, but understanding how to present yourself professionally online, communicate with care, use the right tools for the right purpose, and protect yourself from real threats. Before this project, platforms like GitHub and LinkedIn felt like things I would deal with later in my degree. Setting them up in the first year made me realise that a professional digital identity is something you build gradually, and the earlier you start, the better.
The infographic task taught me to think about design and audience. The portfolio task showed me that even a minimal, well-structured profile is better than no presence at all. HackerRank introduced me to the standard that the industry actually uses to evaluate code, which is very different from simply running it on my own machine. The email etiquette task reinforced that tone and structure matter as much as content when communicating professionally. And the cybercrime research genuinely changed how I will behave online — particularly around UPI and financial requests.
This GitHub repository is the first real entry in what I intend to build into a meaningful portfolio over the next four years. I am glad this course made that feel like something worth starting now.
